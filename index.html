Why Gravity?

This simple question reveals the deepest mystery in science. We can measure gravity's strength to fifteen decimal places. We can predict planetary orbits millennia into the future. We can detect gravitational waves from black holes billions of light-years away. But we cannot answer the most basic question a child might ask: Why does gravity exist at all?

The same mystery pervades all of physics. We excel at measuring what and how—the precise values of fundamental constants, the elegant equations governing motion and energy, the intricate dance of quantum particles. Science has become humanity's most powerful tool for measurement and prediction. Yet for all our mathematical sophistication, we remain profoundly ignorant about why any of it works.

Why do particles have the specific masses they do? Why are there exactly four fundamental forces? Why does the universe operate according to mathematical laws at all? These questions strike at the heart of existence itself, yet traditional physics treats them as forever beyond the reach of scientific inquiry.
