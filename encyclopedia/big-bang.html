In Plain English

Picture reality as a brand‑new, perfectly empty spreadsheet that suddenly comes alive. At tick zero, the sheet gets its grid, the clock starts, and the rules for how entries can move get switched on. Every tick, information can hop one cell to a neighbor—no shortcuts, no instant jumps. That hop‑per‑tick is the universal speed limit.

Because everything wakes up at once, the sheet starts out highly “compressed.” Paths for moving information are crowded and biased. The system’s rule is simple: penalize extremes and reward balance. So it expands—not by flying debris, but by re‑routing: the average spacing between active routes grows to even out the pressure. The result looks, from the inside, like a hot early universe that cools as the ledger spreads its load.

Some paths never quite resolve cleanly. Their unresolved pressure shows up as a steady, gravitating background—the “dark matter” fraction—without needing new particles. And because the first tick synchronized the whole grid, the distant regions we see today started already in step, explaining why the cosmic background looks so uniform without invoking a pre‑existing continuum.
