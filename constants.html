What are constants of nature?

Constants are the fixed conversion factors and invariants that make a universe coherent: the maximum signal speed, the action quantum, the strength of electromagnetic interaction. In traditional physics we measure them and build theories around the numbers. In Recognition Science, we ask a harder question: why must these values be what they are?

A constant is not just a number. It is a constraint the world places on itself so recognition can proceed without contradiction.

We know their measurements—almost never their why

Modern physics achieves extraordinary precision yet keeps dozens of constants as inputs. We fit them to experiment and move on. Recognition Science takes the opposite route: start with what must be true and let logic force the constants. This is the natural sequel to the Eight Theorems: once the ledger exists and evolves in atomic ticks, the allowable scales and ratios narrow to a single consistent family.
