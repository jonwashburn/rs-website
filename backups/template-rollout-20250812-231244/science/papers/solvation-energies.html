A Hierarchical Scaling Model for Solvation Energies in Aqueous Systems

Jonathan Washburn

September 12, 2024

Abstract

We present a novel computational framework for predicting solvation energies in aqueous environments, based on a hierarchical scaling approach that bridges quantum and molecular scales without empirical parameters. The model employs a self-similar scaling factor (\(\phi\)) derived from optimization principles to propagate interactions from a fundamental energy quantum (\(E_{\text{coh}} \approx 0.090\) eV) to macroscopic observables. This framework yields accurate predictions for ion solvation energies (within 5-15\% of experimental values) and acid dissociation constants (mean absolute error \(\sim\)0.5 units), suggesting a unified pathway for multiscale modeling in physical chemistry.

1. A Parameter-Free Model for Solvation

The prediction of solvation free energies is a central challenge in computational chemistry, often relying on empirical parameters or costly simulations. We introduce a new model based on the hierarchical principles of Recognition Science. By starting with a universal energy quantum (\(E_{\text{coh}}\)) and a golden-ratio based scaling law, our framework derives solvation properties from first principles, eliminating the need for empirical fitting.

2. Theoretical Foundation

The model is built on eight foundational axioms that enforce a self-consistent, bounded accounting system for physical interactions. Key features include:

  • Hierarchical Scaling: Interactions propagate from a fundamental length scale across discrete levels using the golden ratio \(\phi\) as a scaling factor. This naturally bridges quantum effects and continuum behavior.
  • Discrete State Machine: A finite automaton with six registers tracks interaction balances, ensuring thermodynamic consistency.
  • Eight-Dimensional Symmetry: An octonion-based symmetry group provides a compact and efficient way to represent complex, multi-component interactions in the solvation shell.

3. Computational Implementation and Results

The model is implemented in an open-source Python package (\texttt{rs_solvation}) designed for efficiency and integration into existing workflows. The total solvation energy is calculated as a sum of cavity formation, electrostatic, and balance terms, all derived from the hierarchical framework.

The model's predictions show strong agreement with experimental data:

  • Ion Solvation Energies: Predictions for alkali and halide ions are within 5-15\% of experimental values, outperforming unparameterized continuum models.
  • pKa Values: For a range of common acids, the model achieves a mean absolute error of \(\sim\)0.5 pKa units, comparable to machine learning methods but without requiring training data.
  • Minimal Interaction Systems: For noble gases, the model correctly predicts near-zero interaction energies due to symmetry-induced cancellations.

4. Conclusion

The hierarchical scaling model provides a powerful, parameter-free framework for predicting solvation properties. Its success in reproducing experimental data for a diverse set of molecules demonstrates the unifying power of the Recognition Science principles. By bridging quantum and macroscopic scales through a consistent, axiomatically-derived system, this work opens new avenues for predictive modeling in chemistry, materials science, and biology, all while reducing the reliance on empirical parameterization.
