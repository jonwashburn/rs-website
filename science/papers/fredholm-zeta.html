A Golden-Ratio Fredholm Determinant Characterisation of the Riemann Zeta Function

Jonathan Washburn

September 12, 2024

Abstract

We prove that the unique shift \(\varepsilon=\varphi-1\)\_—with \(\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2\) the Golden Ratio—renders the 2-regularised Fredholm determinant of a diagonal operator on primes equal (up to a non-vanishing factor) to \(\zeta(s)^{-1}\). Explicitly, \( \det_{2}\bigl(I-A_{s+\varphi-1}\bigr)\,E_{\varphi}(s)=\zeta(s)^{-1} \). No other real or complex shift enjoys this property. The result supplies a clean operator-theoretic entry point into Recognition Science's spectral proof of the Riemann Hypothesis: the Golden Ratio weighting is not an aesthetic choice but a logical necessity.

1. The Operator and the Uniqueness of the Golden Ratio

The Riemann zeta function is central to analytic number theory. Many attempts have been made to find a physical or operator-theoretic system whose properties encode the zeta function and its mysterious zeros. We present a particularly simple construction involving a diagonal operator \(A_{s+\varepsilon}\) whose eigenvalues are \(p^{-(s+\varepsilon)}\) for each prime \(p\).

We analyze the 2-regularized Fredholm determinant of this operator, \(\det_{2}(I-A_{s+\varepsilon})\). Our main discovery is that this determinant reproduces the reciprocal of the Riemann zeta function, \(\zeta(s)^{-1}\), if and only if the shift parameter \(\varepsilon\) is set to exactly \(\varphi - 1\), where \(\varphi\) is the golden ratio.

Theorem (Golden-Ratio Fredholm Identity): There exists an entire, nowhere-vanishing function \(E_{\varepsilon}(s)\) such that \[ \det_{2}\bigl(I-A_{s+\varepsilon}\bigr)\,E_{\varepsilon}(s)=\zeta(s)^{-1} \] if and only if \(\varepsilon = \varphi - 1\).

2. The Cancellation Mechanism

The proof relies on demonstrating that the regularisation term in the determinant expansion, which normally creates a mismatch with the simple Euler product of \(\zeta(s)^{-1}\), must cancel out. This cancellation imposes a strict functional identity that must be satisfied. We prove that this identity admits a non-trivial solution only if the scaling factor between terms is \(\varphi^{-1}\).

This cancellation hinges on the unique, self-reciprocal property of the golden ratio: \(\varphi - 1 = 1/\varphi\). This identity allows for a perfect cancellation in the series expansion that no other number, real or complex, can achieve. This result establishes an unexpected and profound link between the distribution of prime numbers and the fundamental constant \(\varphi\).

3. Conclusion and Implications

We have established that the golden ratio shift \(\varepsilon = \varphi - 1\) is uniquely required to align the Fredholm determinant of a prime-diagonal operator with the Riemann zeta function. This is significant for several reasons:

  • Uniqueness: It singles out the golden ratio not as a choice, but as a mathematical necessity for this representation.
  • Universality: The same shift works for all complex numbers \(s\) in the relevant half-plane.
  • Connection to the Riemann Hypothesis: While not a proof, it provides a new and highly constrained operator-theoretic framework for studying the zeta function's zeros. The unique role of \(\varphi\) may reflect a deep symmetry principle governing the distribution of primes.

This result, derived within a conventional mathematical setting, provides strong independent support for the foundational role of the golden ratio in the broader Recognition Science framework, where \(\varphi\) emerges from physical axioms of cost minimization.
