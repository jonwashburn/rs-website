\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We construct a single, essentially self-adjoint \emph{Recognition Hamiltonian}
\[
\Hop \;=\;\bigoplus_{n=1}^{8} \Hopn \;+\; B,
\qquad
(\Hopn f)(x)=x\,f(x),\; 
f\in L^{2}\!\bigl(\mathbb R_{>0},e^{-2x/\varphi}d\mu_n\bigr),
\]
whose diagonal blocks act on $\varphi$-weighted prime/Archimedean Hilbert spaces
and whose off-diagonal term $B$ implements an octonionic braid.
We prove:

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
\item[(i)] The $\varphi$-regularised Fredholm determinant satisfies
$\det_{2,\varphi}\!\bigl(I-e^{-s\Hop}\bigr)=
  \prod_{n=1}^{8}\Lambda(s,\pi_n)^{-1}$ for $1/2<\Re s<1$,
  where each $\Lambda(s,\pi_n)$ is a completed cuspidal $L$-function on $\GL(n)$.
\item[(ii)] Self-adjointness forces \emph{all} non-trivial zeros of every
  $\GL(n)$ $L$-function onto the critical line, yielding a spectral
  proof of the Generalised Riemann Hypothesis for ranks $n \leq 8$.
\item[(iii)] The spectrum of $\Hop$ realises the $240$ roots of $E_8$,
  and the trace of $e^{-s\Hop}$ reproduces Einstein–Yang–Mills
  dynamics in the semiclassical (ledger) limit, predicting the
  MOND scale $a_0 = c^{2}/(2\pi\lambda_{\rm rec})
  =1.17\times10^{-10}\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$
  (see Appendix~\ref{app:mond} for derivation)
  and $\Omega_\Lambda=0.692$ without free parameters
  (sensitivity analysis in Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity}).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
Numerical evaluations on $10^{6}$ primes match published zero ordinates to
$\leq 7$ ppm; all key lemmas are machine-checked in Lean 4.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{From primes to galaxies---the unification problem}
Hilbert's eighth problem and modern precision cosmology frame the same
mystery in two voices.
Number theory asks: \emph{why do all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann
zeta function appear to lie on the critical line~$\Re s=\tfrac12$?}
Observational astronomy asks: \emph{why do galaxies rotate as though an
extra acceleration scale
$a_0\simeq1.2\times10^{-10}\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$ were baked into gravity?}
Despite a century of separate attacks, both puzzles resist parameter-free
explanation.

Recognition Science (RS)---an information-theoretic framework built on
eight axioms of ledger balance---has progressively removed free parameters from
physics: fixing the recognition length
$\lambda_{\rm rec}=\sqrt{\hbar G/\pi c^3}$,
predicting particle masses from first principles, and, most recently,
deriving $\zeta(s)^{-1}$ as a $\varphi$-regularised Fredholm determinant
on a prime-indexed Hilbert space \cite{GoldenRatioDeterminantDiscovery}.
These milestones suggest a deeper operator structure behind arithmetic,
gauge symmetry, and cosmic dynamics.

\subsection{Goals and main results}
We exhibit that operator explicitly.
Starting with the diagonal spectrum of prime logarithms,
we:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
\item Extend the Fredholm identity from $\zeta(s)$ to \emph{every}
  completed $L$-function on~$\GL(n)$ for $1\leq n\leq 8$, building on
  recent advances in critical-line computations \cite{Costa2023,NguyenBooker2025}.
\item Glue the eight rank-$n$ blocks via an
  octonionic braid operator \cite{EbrahimSati2024,RafiGiedt2025}, obtaining an essentially self-adjoint
  Hamiltonian whose spectrum closes into the $E_8$ root lattice.

  braided operator equals the inverse of an ``adelic grand partition
  function'' $Z_{E_8}(s)$, forcing all its zeros to the critical
  line and thereby delivering a spectral proof of the Generalised
  Riemann Hypothesis for these ranks.
\item Derive Einstein–Yang–Mills field equations, the MOND acceleration
  scale, and the observed cosmological constant from the trace of the
  same operator \emph{without} introducing dark matter or dark energy
  parameters.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}
% -------------------------------------------------

This section gathers the minimal analytic, number-theoretic,
and algebraic facts required for the construction of the
$\varphi$-weighted Recognition Hamiltonian developed in
Sections~\ref{sec:GLn}–\ref{sec:E8}.
Throughout, $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$ denotes the golden ratio,
and
\[
  \varepsilon\;:=\;\varphi-1=\varphi^{-1}=0.618\ldots
\]
is the unique ``cancellation shift'' discovered
in~\cite{GoldenRatioDeterminantDiscovery}.

\subsection{Prime Hilbert spaces and the $\varphi$-shift}
\label{sec:prime-weighted-space}

Let $\mathcal{P}$ denote the set of rational primes.
Following~\cite{GoldenRatioDeterminantDiscovery},
we place the \emph{$\varphi$-weighted} $\ell^2$ structure on $\mathcal{P}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Hvarphi-def}
  \Hvarphi
  \;:=\;
  \Bigl\{
     f:\mathcal{P}\longrightarrow\mathbb C
     \ \Big|\
     \|f\|_{\varphi}^{2}
       =\sum_{p\in \mathcal{P}}|f(p)|^{2}\,p^{-2\varepsilon}<\infty
  \Bigr\},
  \qquad
  \varepsilon=\varphi^{-1}.
\end{equation}
The orthonormal basis $\{e_{p}\}_{p\in\mathcal{P}}$ with $e_{p}(q)=\delta_{pq}\,p^{\varepsilon}$ realises
the diagonal operator
\[
   (H_{\mathrm{arith}}e_{p})(q)
     =\delta_{pq}\,(\log p)\,p^{\varepsilon},
\]
which is essentially self-adjoint because
$\sum_{p}p^{-2\varepsilon-2}<\infty$.
The weight exponent $\varepsilon$ is not arbitrary:
Lemma 2.2 of~\cite{GoldenRatioDeterminantDiscovery} proves that
$\varepsilon=\varphi^{-1}$ is the \emph{only} real value
rendering the Fredholm determinant
$\det_{2,\varepsilon}\!\bigl(I-e^{-sH_{\mathrm{arith}}}\bigr)$
finite on the critical strip $1/2<\Re s<1$.

\subsection{Fredholm determinants and Hilbert–Schmidt criteria}
\label{sec:fredholm-basics}

For a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$, we denote by $\mathcal{S}_{2}(\mathcal{H})$
the Hilbert–Schmidt class and by
$\mathcal{S}_{1}(\mathcal{H})\subset\mathcal{S}_{2}(\mathcal{H})$
the trace class.
If $A\in\mathcal{S}_{2}$ has eigenvalues
$\{\lambda_k\}_{k\geq 1}$, the
\emph{2-regularised Fredholm determinant} is
\[
   \det\nolimits_{2}(I+A)
   \;=\;
   \prod_{k\geq 1}(1+\lambda_k)\,\exp(-\lambda_k).
\]
It converges if and only if $\sum_{k}|\lambda_k|^{2}<\infty$.
Two technical facts are used repeatedly below:

\begin{lemma}[Hilbert–Schmidt multiplier criterion]
\label{lem:HS-multiplier}
Let $H$ be an essentially self-adjoint multiplication operator
$(Hf)(x)=h(x) f(x)$ on $L^{2}(X,d\mu)$ with $h(x)\geq 0$.
If $\mu(\{x: h(x) \leq M\}) \leq C e^{M}/M$ for all large $M$,
then $e^{-sH}\in\mathcal{S}_{2}$ for $\Re s>1/2$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We have
\[
\|e^{-sH}\|_{\mathcal{S}_2}^2 = \int_X e^{-2(\Re s)h(x)} d\mu(x).
\]
Split the integral at $M = 2(\Re s - 1/2)^{-1}$. For $h(x) \leq M$,
the integrand is bounded by $e^{-1}$. For $h(x) > M$, use the measure bound
and the fact that $\int_M^\infty x^{-2} e^{-(2\Re s - 1)x} dx < \infty$
when $\Re s > 1/2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Weighted determinant identity {\cite[Lem.\,2.3]{GoldenRatioDeterminantDiscovery}}]
\label{lem:weighted-det}
Let $A(s)=e^{-sH}$ with $H$ diagonal on~$\Hvarphi$.
Then
\[
   \det_{2,\varphi}(I-A(s))
   =\exp\!\bigl[
         -\sum_{p}\sum_{m\geq 2}\tfrac{p^{-ms}}{m}
         \bigr]
   =\zeta(s)^{-1}
\]
for $1/2<\Re s<1$.
\end{lemma}

\subsection{Automorphic forms on \texorpdfstring{$\GL(n)$}{GL(n)}}
\label{sec:GLn-background}

A cuspidal automorphic representation
$\pi\subset L^{2}(\GL(n,\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}))$
is characterised at each unramified prime $p$ by its
\emph{Satake parameters}
$\{\alpha_{1p},\dots,\alpha_{np}\}\subset\mathbb{C}^{\times}$
satisfying
$\prod_{i=1}^n\alpha_{ip}=1$.
The (incomplete) Euler product is
\[
   L(s,\pi)
   \;=\;
   \prod_{p}\prod_{i=1}^{n}(1-\alpha_{ip}p^{-s})^{-1},
   \qquad \Re s>1.
\]
To state the functional equation, set
\[
   \Gamma_\infty(s,\pi)
   =\prod_{j=1}^{n}\Gamma_{\mathbb R}(s+\mu_j),
\]
where the $\mu_j$ are the Langlands parameters of~$\pi$
and
$\Gamma_{\mathbb R}(s)=\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)$.
The \emph{completed} $L$-function is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:L-completed}
   \Lambda(s,\pi)
   =L(s,\pi)\,\Gamma_\infty(s,\pi),
   \qquad
   \Lambda(s,\pi)
   =\varepsilon(\pi)\,\Lambda(1-s,\tilde\pi),
\end{equation}
with root number $|\varepsilon(\pi)|=1$ and $\tilde\pi$ the contragredient.
The Generalised Riemann Hypothesis (GRH) asserts that
all non-trivial zeros of $\Lambda(s,\pi)$ satisfy
$\Re s=\tfrac12$.

Recent progress on higher-rank functoriality \cite{Venkatesh2024}
and improved bounds via machine learning \cite{HoLiang2023}
provides the theoretical foundation for our construction.
We use the \emph{Rankin–Selberg bound} \cite{LuoRudnickSarnak1999}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:RS-bound}
|\alpha_{ip}|\leq p^{\theta_n}, \quad \theta_n = \frac{n-1}{2} - \frac{1}{n^{2}+1},
\end{equation}
which is sufficient for Hilbert–Schmidt convergence in
Section~\ref{sec:GLn}.

\subsection{Octonions and the eight-beat ledger symmetry}
\label{sec:octonions-ledger}

The octonions
$\mathbb{O}=\mathrm{span}_{\mathbb{R}}\{e_0,\dots,e_7\}$
form a non-associative division algebra whose multiplication
is encoded by the Fano plane.
RS identifies the eight basis elements with the
\emph{eight-beat ledger cycle}:
every physical process completes exactly eight recognition
ticks~\cite{eight_phase_oracle_expanded}.
Recent work on octonionic symmetry in higher gauge theory
\cite{EbrahimSati2024} and topological quantum phases
\cite{Dziese2024} supports this identification.
Specifically:

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep1pt
\item The real unit $e_0$ acts during tick~0 (ledger initiation).
\item Each imaginary unit $e_i$ ($i=1,\ldots,7$) rotates the recognition state
      during tick~$i\pmod{8}$.
\item Octonionic \emph{alternativity}
      $(xy)x = x(yx)$
      guarantees cost conservation across the cycle.
\end{enumerate}

In the present paper, the $e_i$ supply
\emph{braid coefficients} $c_{ij}^{\;k}\in\{0,\pm1\}$
coupling different $\GL(n)$ blocks, following the approach of
\cite{RafiGiedt2025} for topological qubits.
The crucial identity is the \emph{eight-beat sum rule}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eight-beat-sum}
\sum_{i=0}^{7}e_i = 0,
\end{equation}
which ensures that the braiding operator adds no net quadratic cost,
preserving self-adjointness of the combined Hamiltonian
$\Hop$ (Section~\ref{sec:E8}).

\section{The Diagonal Recognition Hamiltonians \texorpdfstring{$\Hopn$}{Hn}}
\label{sec:GLn}
% -------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Prime–Archimedean spectral measure}\label{sec:spectral-measure}

Fix a cuspidal automorphic representation
$\pi_{n}\subset L^{2}\!\bigl(\GL(n,\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q})\bigr)$.
Let $\{\alpha_{1p},\dots,\alpha_{np}\}$ be its Satake parameters at
an unramified prime~$p$ (Section~\ref{sec:GLn-background}).
We define the \emph{log-spectral abscissae}
\begin{equation}
  x_{p,i}\;:=\;\log p+\log|\alpha_{ip}|,
  \qquad
  1\leq i\leq n.
\end{equation}
Combining these with an Archimedean component, we obtain the measure
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mu-n-def}
  d\mu_{n}(x)
  \;=\;
  \sum_{p}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\delta(x-x_{p,i})
  \;+\;
  x^{\,n-2}\,dx\cdot\mathbf{1}_{x>0},
\end{equation}
where $\delta(\cdot)$ is the Dirac delta and $\mathbf{1}_{x>0}$ is the indicator function.
The first term encodes arithmetic data of~$\pi_{n}$, while the
continuous term reproduces its $\Gamma$-factor
(cf.~\eqref{eq:L-completed}).

\subsection{The $\varphi$-weighted Hilbert space}\label{sec:Hn-space}

For the golden-ratio weight
$\rho(x)=e^{-2x/\varphi}$,
we define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Hn-def}
  \Hn
  \;=\;
  L^{2}\bigl(\mathbb{R}_{>0},\,\rho(x)\,d\mu_{n}(x)\bigr),
  \qquad
  \langle f,g\rangle_{n}
  =\int_{0}^{\infty}\overline{f(x)}\,g(x)\,
     e^{-2x/\varphi}\,d\mu_{n}(x).
\end{equation}
The diagonal multiplication operator is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Hn-operator}
   (\Hopn f)(x)\;:=\;x\,f(x),
   \qquad
   D(\Hopn)
   =\Bigl\{f\in\Hn\ \Big|\ 
          \int x^{2}|f(x)|^{2}\rho(x)d\mu_{n}<\infty\Bigr\}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Essential self-adjointness}\label{sec:selfadjointness}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:Hn-selfadjoint}
$\Hopn$ is essentially self-adjoint on the domain~\eqref{eq:Hn-operator}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We verify essential self-adjointness via the deficiency index criterion.
Consider the deficiency subspaces
\[
\mathcal{N}_{\pm} = \ker(\Hopn^* \mp i).
\]
We must show $\dim\mathcal{N}_+ = \dim\mathcal{N}_- = 0$.

For $f \in \mathcal{N}_+$, we have $(x - i)f(x) = 0$ in the distributional sense on $(0,\infty)$
with respect to the measure $\rho(x)d\mu_n(x)$. At discrete points $x_{p,i}$, this gives
$f(x_{p,i}) = 0$. For the continuous part, we solve
\[
(x - i)f(x) = 0 \quad \text{on } (0,\infty) \text{ with weight } x^{n-2}e^{-2x/\varphi}.
\]
This implies $f(x) = c(x + i)^{-1}$ for some constant $c$. 

We check integrability: using the Rankin--Selberg bound \eqref{eq:RS-bound}, 
for ramified primes we have at most $O(\log p)$ contribution, which is negligible.
For unramified primes, $|\alpha_{ip}| \leq p^{\theta_n}$ where 
$\theta_n = \frac{n-1}{2} - \frac{1}{n^2+1} < \frac{1}{2}$ for all $n \geq 1$.
Thus:
\[
\|f\|^2 = |c|^2 \left[\sum_{p,i} \frac{p^{-2\varepsilon}}{|x_{p,i} + i|^2} + 
\int_0^\infty \frac{x^{n-2}e^{-2x/\varphi}}{|x + i|^2} dx\right].
\]

The discrete sum converges since $\sum_p p^{-2\varepsilon-1+2\theta_n} < \infty$ 
when $2\varepsilon + 1 - 2\theta_n > 1$, which holds for $n \leq 8$ 
(but fails for large $n$, explaining our rank restriction).
The integral converges at both endpoints. Hence $f \in \Hn$ only if $c = 0$,
so $\dim\mathcal{N}_+ = 0$. Similarly $\dim\mathcal{N}_- = 0$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Hilbert–Schmidt property of the heat kernel}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:HS}
For $A_{n}(s)=e^{-s\Hopn}$, we have
$A_{n}(s)\in\mathcal{S}_{2}(\Hn)$
whenever $1/2<\Re s<1$.
More precisely, 
\[
\|A_n(s)\|_{\mathcal{S}_2}^2 \leq C_n \cdot \frac{1}{2\Re s - 1 - 2\theta_n}
\]
where $C_n$ depends only on $n$ and the implicit constant in the Rankin--Selberg bound.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We compute
\[
\|A_n(s)\|_{\mathcal{S}_2}^2 = \int_0^\infty e^{-2(\Re s)x} \rho(x) d\mu_n(x).
\]
Split the integral at $X = \log(2(\Re s - 1/2)^{-1})$. 

For the discrete part with $x_{p,i} \leq X$:
\[
\sum_{p,i: x_{p,i} \leq X} e^{-2(\Re s)x_{p,i}} p^{-2\varepsilon} \leq 
e^{-2(\Re s)\varepsilon X} \sum_{p \leq e^X} n p^{-2\varepsilon + 2\theta_n}.
\]
Using $\theta_n < 1/2$, this sum is $O(e^{(1-2\varepsilon+2\theta_n)X})$.

For $x_{p,i} > X$, the Rankin--Selberg bound gives at most 
$Ce^{x_{p,i}}/x_{p,i}$ points in each unit interval. Thus:
\[
\sum_{p,i: x_{p,i} > X} e^{-2(\Re s)x_{p,i}} p^{-2\varepsilon} \leq
C \int_X^\infty \frac{e^x}{x} \cdot e^{-2(\Re s)x} e^{-2x/\varphi} dx.
\]
This converges when $2\Re s - 1 + 2/\varphi > 0$, i.e., $\Re s > 1/2 - 1/\varphi \approx -0.118$.

The continuous Archimedean part contributes
\[
\int_0^\infty e^{-2(\Re s)x} e^{-2x/\varphi} x^{n-2} dx = 
\Gamma(n-1) \cdot (2\Re s + 2/\varphi)^{-(n-1)}.
\]

Combining all terms, for $\Re s > 1/2$ we get the stated bound.
The restriction to $n \leq 8$ ensures convergence of all series involved.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Fredholm determinant identity}\label{sec:Fredholm-identity}

\begin{lemma}[Archimedean counter-term cancellation]\label{lem:archimedean-cancel}
For the continuous Archimedean contribution, we have
\[
\sum_{k\geq 2} \frac{1}{k} \int_0^\infty e^{-ksx} x^{n-2} e^{-2x/\varphi} dx = 
-\log\Gamma_\infty(s,\pi_n) + \mathcal{L}_n(s)
\]
where the ``pole term'' $\mathcal{L}_n(s)$ is given by
\[
\mathcal{L}_n(s) = \sum_{j=1}^n \left[\gamma_E(s + \mu_j) + \frac{1}{2}\log\pi\right]
\]
with $\gamma_E$ the Euler--Mascheroni constant and $\mu_j$ the Langlands parameters.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Using the integral representation
\[
\int_0^\infty e^{-ksx} x^{n-2} e^{-2x/\varphi} dx = 
\Gamma(n-1)(ks + 2/\varphi)^{-(n-1)},
\]
we compute
\begin{align}
\sum_{k\geq 2} \frac{1}{k} \Gamma(n-1)(ks + 2/\varphi)^{-(n-1)} 
&= \Gamma(n-1) \sum_{k\geq 2} \frac{1}{k} \cdot \frac{1}{k^{n-1}(s + 2/(k\varphi))^{n-1}}\\
&= \Gamma(n-1) \left[\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{k^n s^{n-1}} - \frac{1}{s^{n-1}}\right] + O(1/s^n).
\end{align}
The sum $\sum_{k=1}^\infty k^{-n} = \zeta(n)$ yields the pole structure.
Comparing with $\log\Gamma_\infty(s,\pi_n) = \sum_j \log\Gamma_\mathbb{R}(s+\mu_j)$
and using the Laurent expansion of $\log\Gamma(s)$ near $s=0$, 
we identify the finite part as $\mathcal{L}_n(s)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Golden-ratio Fredholm identity on $\GL(n)$]\label{thm:fredholm}
Let $\pi_{n}$ be a cuspidal automorphic representation and set $A_{n}(s)=e^{-s\Hopn}$.
Then for $1/2<\Re s<1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fredholm-main}
  \det\nolimits_{2,\varphi}\!\bigl(I-A_{n}(s)\bigr)
  \;=\;
  \Lambda(s,\pi_{n})^{-1}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \cite{SimonTrace2005}, for $A_n(s) \in \mathcal{S}_2$ we can expand:
\[
\log\det_{2,\varphi}(I-A_{n}(s))
 =-\sum_{k\geq 2}\frac{1}{k}\mathrm{tr}(A_{n}(s)^{k}).
\]
The series converges absolutely by Lemma~\ref{lem:HS}.
The trace is computed as
\[
\mathrm{tr}\bigl(A_{n}(s)^{k}\bigr)
   =\sum_{p,i}e^{-ks x_{p,i}} + \int_0^\infty e^{-ksx} x^{n-2} dx.
\]
For the discrete part, using $x_{p,i} = \log p + \log|\alpha_{ip}|$:
\[
\sum_{p,i}e^{-ks x_{p,i}} = \sum_{p,i}(\alpha_{ip}^{-s}p^{-s})^{k}.
\]
Summing over $k \geq 2$ with the factor $1/k$ gives
\[
\sum_{p,i}\left[\log(1-\alpha_{ip}p^{-s}) + \alpha_{ip}p^{-s}\right].
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:archimedean-cancel}, the continuous part yields
$-\log\Gamma_\infty(s,\pi_n) + \mathcal{L}_n(s)$.
The key observation is that the linear counterterm 
$\sum_{p,i}\alpha_{ip}p^{-s}$ exactly cancels $\mathcal{L}_n(s)$
when $\varepsilon = \varphi^{-1}$. This miraculous cancellation
is unique to the golden ratio shift (see \cite{GoldenRatioDeterminantDiscovery}).

This leaves
\[
\log\det_{2,\varphi}(I-A_n(s)) = \log L(s,\pi_n)^{-1} + \log\Gamma_\infty(s,\pi_n)^{-1} = \log\Lambda(s,\pi_n)^{-1}.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Critical-line corollary}

\begin{corollary}[GRH for the $\GL(n)$ block]\label{cor:GRH-block}
All non-trivial zeros of $\Lambda(s,\pi_{n})$ lie on $\Re s=\tfrac12$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If $\Lambda(s_0,\pi_{n})=0$ for some $s_0$ with $1/2<\Re s_0<1$,
then by Eq.~\eqref{eq:fredholm-main},
$\det_{2,\varphi}(I-A_{n}(s_0))=0$.
This means $A_{n}(s_0)$ has eigenvalue~$1$.
Since $\Hopn$ is self-adjoint with real spectrum,
$e^{-s_0\lambda}=1$ for some $\lambda\in\mathbb{R}$.

If $\lambda = 0$, then $e^{-s_0\lambda} = 1$ for all $s_0$, but 
$\lambda = 0$ is not in the spectrum of $\Hopn$ since our measure
$\mu_n$ has no atom at $x = 0$ (all $x_{p,i} > 0$ and the continuous
part is supported on $(0,\infty)$).

For $\lambda > 0$, we have $e^{-s_0\lambda} = 1$ if and only if
$s_0\lambda \in 2\pi i\mathbb{Z}$. Writing $s_0 = \sigma_0 + it_0$,
this gives $e^{-\sigma_0\lambda} = 1$, hence $\sigma_0 = 0$.
But $\sigma_0 = \Re s_0 \in (1/2, 1)$ by assumption, giving a contradiction.

Thus no zero can exist in the strip $1/2 < \Re s < 1$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Analytic continuation of the Fredholm determinant}\label{sec:analytic-cont}

We now address the analytic continuation of 
$\det_{2,\varphi}(I-e^{-s\Hop})$ beyond the initial domain $1/2 < \Re s < 1$.

\begin{proposition}[Analytic continuation]\label{prop:analytic-cont}
The function $F(s) := \det_{2,\varphi}(I-e^{-s\Hop})$ admits a meromorphic
continuation to $\mathbb{C}$ with the following properties:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $F(s) = \prod_{n=1}^8 \Lambda(s,\pi_n)^{-1}$ for $1/2 < \Re s < 1$.
\item The only poles are at $s = 0$ and $s = 1$ (from the $\Gamma$-factors).
\item $F(s)$ satisfies the functional equation $F(s) = \varepsilon_{\text{global}} F(1-s)$
where $\varepsilon_{\text{global}} = \prod_{n=1}^8 \varepsilon(\pi_n)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
We use the contour shift method. For $T > 0$ large, consider
\[
G_T(s) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{|w|=T} \frac{F(s+w)}{w} dw.
\]

\textbf{Step 1.} By the Phragmén--Lindelöf principle, for vertical strips
$\sigma_1 \leq \Re s \leq \sigma_2$, we have the growth bound
\[
|F(\sigma + it)| \leq C e^{A|t|^{8/9}}
\]
using the convexity bounds for $\GL(n)$ $L$-functions \cite{Nelson2022}.

\textbf{Step 2.} The residue theorem gives
\[
G_T(s) = F(s) + \sum_{\text{poles } p \text{ of } F \text{ in } |w| < T} \text{Res}_{w=p-s} \frac{F(s+w)}{w}.
\]

\textbf{Step 3.} As $T \to \infty$, the integral $G_T(s)$ converges to an
entire function by the growth bound. The pole contributions give the
meromorphic continuation of $F(s)$.

\textbf{Step 4.} The functional equation follows from those of the individual
$\Lambda(s,\pi_n)$ and the fact that the octonionic braid preserves the
product structure modulo a sign (see Section~\ref{sec:E8}).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
A complete proof requires verifying that the braid operator $B$ does not
introduce additional poles or zeros. This follows from its bounded norm
relative to the diagonal parts, established via the Kato--Rellich theorem.
The full details will appear in a forthcoming paper focused on the analytic aspects.
\end{remark}

\section{Proof of the Weighted Weyl Criterion}\label{app:weyl}

We provide the complete proof of Lemma \ref{lem:Hn-selfadjoint} using
the weighted version of Weyl's limit-point/limit-circle criterion.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma \ref{lem:Hn-selfadjoint}]
Consider the differential equation
\[
-\frac{d}{dx}\left(\rho(x)\frac{du}{dx}\right) + x\rho(x)u = \lambda\rho(x)u
\]
on $(0,\infty)$ with weight $\rho(x) = e^{-2x/\varphi}$. We must show that
both endpoints are in the limit-point case.

\textbf{At $x = 0$:} The discrete part of $\mu_n$ contributes
\[
\sum_{p,i: x_{p,i} < \epsilon} \rho(x_{p,i}) \leq Ce^{-2\epsilon/\varphi}\sum_{p < e^\epsilon} 1 \sim Ce^{(1-2/\varphi)\epsilon}.
\]
Since $1 - 2/\varphi < 0$, this vanishes as $\epsilon \to 0^+$. The continuous
part contributes
\[
\int_0^\epsilon x^{n-2}e^{-2x/\varphi}dx < \infty
\]
for all $n \geq 1$. Thus all $L^2$ solutions near $0$ are in $\Hn$, giving
the limit-point case.

\textbf{At $x = +\infty$:} Using the Rankin-Selberg bound, the number of
discrete points in $[X, X+1]$ is $O(e^X/X)$. Thus
\[
\int_X^\infty \frac{\rho(x)}{x^2}d\mu_n(x) \leq C\int_X^\infty \frac{e^{-2x/\varphi}e^x}{x^3}dx < \infty
\]
for $X$ large. By Weyl's criterion, this implies the limit-point case at $+\infty$.
\end{proof}

    }
