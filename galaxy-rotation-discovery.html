Galaxy Rotation, No Dark Matter:
How Bandwidth Limits May Rewrite Gravity

A new "information-limited gravity" model claims galaxies rotate the way they do not because of invisible matter, but because the universe updates gravitational fields with finite bandwidth. A reproducible analysis across 126 galaxies puts the idea to the test—and sets clear ways to prove it wrong.

A Galaxy That Shouldn't Spin This Fast

In the 1970s, astronomer Vera Rubin peered through her spectrograph at the Andromeda galaxy and saw something that shouldn't exist. Stars at the galaxy's edge were whipping around at nearly the same speed as those near the center—defying Newton's laws. It was like discovering that Pluto orbits the Sun as fast as Mercury.

For five decades, physicists have explained this cosmic speed limit violation by adding invisible "dark matter" to their equations. But what if the problem isn't missing matter? What if gravity itself works differently when stretched across cosmic scales?

A Different Answer: Information-Limited Gravity

Imagine reality as a vast cosmic spreadsheet that must keep all its cells—every particle, every field—perfectly synchronized. This universal ledger has finite processing power. Like a video streaming service during peak hours, it must prioritize where to allocate its bandwidth.

Solar Systems

Small, fast-changing, high priority. The ledger keeps up—Newton works perfectly.

Galaxies

Vast, slow-moving, lower priority. Update lag creates stronger apparent gravity.

The Pattern

The slower the system evolves, the more lag accumulates, the stronger gravity appears.

The researchers tested their model on the SPARC dataset—high-quality rotation curves for 126 galaxies spanning five orders of magnitude in mass. Unlike typical analyses that adjust parameters for each galaxy, this study used globally fixed values derived from theoretical principles.

How It Works (Without Scaring the Reader)

The model introduces a "weight function" that modifies gravity based on how long a system takes to complete its motions. A planet orbiting in years gets nearly instant updates. A star taking millions of years to circle a galaxy? That's where the lag shows up.

For the Mathematically Curious

The weight function w(r) depends on:

  • Dynamical time (how long orbits take)
  • A universal radial profile
  • System complexity (gas content, morphology)

All parameters are fixed globally from theoretical constraints—no fitting to individual galaxies.

If It's Right: The Big Picture

If information-limited gravity proves correct, it would unify multiple cosmic mysteries under one mechanism. Galaxy rotation, gravitational lensing anomalies, and possibly even dark energy could all emerge from the same bandwidth constraints. It would mean the universe isn't missing 95% of its matter—we've just been using incomplete equations.

Skeptics' Corner

The model faces significant challenges. Its relativistic extension predicts lensing enhancements that haven't been observed. The cosmological implications remain unexplored. And rotation curve fits, while good, rely on systematic uncertainties that could shift the results.

Most importantly, extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. A model that discards dark matter—which successfully explains so many observations—needs to clear a very high bar.

What's Next

The beauty of this proposal is its falsifiability. Within 2-3 years, ongoing experiments will either validate or refute its core predictions. JWST's lensing observations, upgraded pulsar timing arrays, and next-generation laboratory gravity tests all offer ways to test the information-bandwidth hypothesis.
