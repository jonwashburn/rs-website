1) Context and claim

We recast RH as a bounded‑real problem on the half‑plane: construct \(J(s)=\det_2(I-A(s))/\xi(s)\) and its Cayley transform \(\Theta=(2J-1)/(2J+1)\). Proving \(|\Theta(s)|\le1\) on \(\{\Re s>\tfrac12\}\) yields RH. This reframes the classical zero‑free region program in a control‑theoretic, positivity‑first language.

2) The half‑plane program

The right half‑plane \(\Omega=\{\Re s>\tfrac12\}\) is equipped with the Szegő kernel \(B(s,\overline t)=(s+\overline t-1)^{-1}\). Schur/Herglotz classes and Pick kernels are naturally formulated here. This geometry is the stage on which boundary positivity implies interior control via maximum‑principle arguments.

3) Prime‑diagonal operator and HS control

Define the prime‑diagonal operator \(A(s)e_p=p^{-s}e_p\). For \(\sigma=\Re s>1/2\), one has \(\|A(s)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}^2=\sum_{p}p^{-2\sigma}<\infty\) and \(\|A(s)\|<1\). This pins the infinite‑prime contribution inside the Hilbert–Schmidt world where \(\det_2\) is continuous.

4) Regularized determinants and the ξ ratio

We work with \(\det_2\) to factor out linear terms and keep HS control. The ratio \(J(s)=\det_2(I-A(s))/\xi(s)\) normalizes archimedean and pole data into a finite block, isolating the genuinely infinite "prime tail" in a stable way.

5) Cayley transform to Schur

The bounded‑real lens is \(\Theta=(2J-1)/(2J+1)\). Establishing \(|\Theta|\le1\) is equivalent to \(J\) being Herglotz and to positivity of the associated Pick kernel. This bridges analytic number theory to contractive systems.

6) Schur–determinant splitting

For a block operator \(T=\begin{bmatrix}A&B\\ C&D\end{bmatrix}\), the identity \(\log\det_2(I-T)=\log\det_2(I-A)+\log\det(I-S)\) with \(S=D-C(I-A)^{-1}B\) separates HS \((k\ge2)\) from finite \((k=1)\) contributions. This is the algebraic backbone of the approach.

7) HS continuity and prime truncations

Prime truncations \(A_N\to A\) in HS on compacta imply \(\det_2(I-A_N)\to\det_2(I-A)\) locally uniformly. This makes the approximation pipeline compatible with analytic limits in \(\Omega\).

8) Prime‑grid lossless models and KYP closure

Finite‑stage systems tied to primes admit lossless (passive) realizations. Diagonal Lyapunov witnesses factor the KYP matrix, certifying \(\|H_N\|_\infty\le1\). These certificates are the finite anchors for the limiting Schur claim.

9) Additive/log kernel positivity

Using a backward‑difference identity, the logarithmic kernel underlying \(\log\det_2^N\) is shown PSD on boundaries of zero‑free rectangles. This is the analytic engine that drives boundary positivity.

10) Symmetric‑Fock exponential lift aligned with Szegő

We construct a Fock‑type exponential kernel \(E_N\) lower‑bounded by Szegő in finite matrices. This alignment lets positivity of the logarithmic Gram lift to the kernel geometry of \(\Omega\).

11) Boundary positivity ⇒ interior Schur

From PSD on \(\partial R\), a maximum‑principle step gives \(\Re J\ge0\) inside \(R\). Cayley then yields \(|\Theta|\le1\) on \(R\). Exhausting \(\Omega\) by rectangles and passing to limits delivers the global claim.

12) Punctured boundaries and Blaschke compensation

Zeros of \(\xi\) on \(\partial R\) are neutralized by a half‑plane Blaschke factor; we work on punctured boundaries, preserve PSD under Schur products, and remove the compensation at the end.

13) Quantitative Poisson–Carleson certificate (P+)

A constructive boundary positive‑real certificate (with explicit window‑dependent constants) closes the boundary route. This is where quantitative harmonic analysis enters.

14) Windowing, Whitney scales, and bandlimits

Smooth windows control localization; Whitney scales and bandlimits tame prime sums and transforms. These design choices keep every contribution uniformly bounded where needed.

15) Explicit archimedean bounds

We give clean bounds for the archimedean part (Γ/digamma) on lines \(\Re s=1/2+\varepsilon\), uniform in \(\varepsilon\), keeping the boundary analysis quantitative.

16) Prime‑side short‑sum control

A precise inequality for the prime contribution is obtained via band‑limited test functions, Plancherel, and PNT‑weak estimates. This secures the prime‑side of the boundary balance.

17) Hilbert transform pairing and BV windows

With bounded‑variation windows, the Hilbert transform obeys explicit \(L^\infty\) bounds. This underwrites the key pairing estimates that appear in the local boundary theorem.

18) Exhaustion and removable singularities

We extract diagonal subsequences on an exhaustion of \(\Omega\setminus Z(\xi)\), obtain local‑uniform convergence in the Schur class, and cross zero sets by removable singularity arguments.

19) Equivalence back to RH

Global Schur/PSD of \(\Theta\) forbids zeros of \(\xi\) with \(\Re s>1/2\) by a maximum‑modulus pinch. This closes the circle from bounded‑real control to the classical statement of RH.

20) Axiomatic Bridging perspective

The Recognition Physics ledger enforces symmetry, conservation, and fairness — principles mirrored as passivity, contractivity, and positivity in this analytic setting. The axiomatic bridging method explains not just the steps we take, but why this route exists and coheres.

What it is

The proof casts RH as a bounded‑real problem on the right half‑plane. Define the prime‑diagonal operator \(A(s)e_p = p^{-s} e_p\), the completed zeta \(\xi(s)\), and the Hilbert–Schmidt regularized determinant \(\det_2\). Setting \(J(s)=\det_2(I-A(s))/\xi(s)\) and \(\Theta(s)=(2J-1)/(2J+1)\), RH follows from Schur positivity: \(|\Theta(s)|\le 1\) on \(\Re s>\tfrac12\).

How we accomplished it

Using axiomatic bridging, we translate the recognition‑ledger primitives into operator constraints, then execute a control‑theoretic program: realize prime‑grid lossless finite stages, certify passivity by KYP, control \(\det_2\) limits in the Hilbert–Schmidt topology, and conclude Schur positivity in the limit.

Method pipeline

1

Schur–determinant split

Block‑factorization: \(\log\det_2(I-T)=\log\det_2(I-A)+\log\det(I-S)\). Separates \(k\ge 2\) (HS) terms from the finite \(k=1\)+archimedean block.

2

HS→\(\det_2\) continuity

Prime truncations \(A_N\to A\) in HS imply local‑uniform convergence of \(\det_2(I-A_N)\) on \(\{\Re s>\tfrac12\}\).

3

Prime‑grid lossless models

Finite‑stage passive realizations tied to primes (diagonal templates, exact/k‑fold blocks) with lossless KYP certificates.

4

Boundary control

Uniform‑in‑\(\varepsilon\) local \(L^1\) theorem via a smoothed estimate for \(\partial_\sigma\Re\log\det_2(I-A)\) and de‑smoothing. Outer neutralization → unimodular boundary values.

5

Alignment and closure

Finite stages align with the \(\det_2\) target (Cayley difference bound). The Schur class is closed under local‑uniform limits → \(|\Theta|\le1\).

Notation: \(\Theta=(H-1)/(H+1)\), \(H=2\,\det_2(I-A)/\xi-1\). HS = Hilbert–Schmidt, KYP = Kalman–Yakubovich–Popov.

Strategies and innovations

Exact k=1 factor as a finite block

We isolate the Euler \(k=1\) term and archimedean/pole pieces into a finite Schur complement that stays contractive on \(\{\Re s\ge \sigma_0\}\).

Lossless KYP with diagonal witnesses

Prime‑grid realizations with diagonal Lyapunov certificates yield explicit lossless equalities and \(\|H_N\|_\infty\le1\).

Uniform local boundary theorem

A direct, unconditional smoothing bound on \(\partial_\sigma\Re\log\det_2(I-A)\) gives a uniform‑in‑\(\varepsilon\) local \(L^1\) limit after de‑smoothing.

Schur/PSD closure under limits

With HS control and alignment, the Schur class persists in the limit — delivering \(|\Theta(s)|\le1\) for \(\Re s>\tfrac12\).
