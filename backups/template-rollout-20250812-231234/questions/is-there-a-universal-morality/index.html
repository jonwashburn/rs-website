The Hard Problem

For millennia, humanity has debated whether moral principles are universal truths or merely cultural conventions. This is the classic battle between moral absolutism and moral relativism. Is there a "true north" for right and wrong that applies to all conscious beings, for all time? Or are our ethics simply a product of our biology, culture, and personal opinions, with no ultimate objective foundation?

The Conventional View

The dominant modern view, particularly in secular thought, leans toward moral relativism. Morality is often seen as a set of useful social rules that evolved to promote cooperation and survival. Different cultures have different rules, and there is no objective standard by which to judge one as superior to another. Religious frameworks propose a universal morality based on divine command, but this requires faith and is not empirically verifiable. The result is a stalemate: ethics remains in the realm of opinion, debate, and power struggles, not objective fact.
