Abstract

Charles Darwin's theory of natural selection, while foundational, leaves the origin of life's fundamental molecular architectures largely explained by contingency. We propose a complementary perspective from Recognition Physics (RP), arguing that core structures like DNA are not accidental but are optimal solutions dictated by universal principles of recognition efficiency. Applying RP's Minimal Overhead principle, we demonstrate the physical necessity of DNA's φ-based geometry. This derived optimal structure inherently maximizes information fidelity, energy efficiency, and quantum coherence—properties crucial for life. This "Recognition Optimization" principle establishes the primary blueprint of life based on physical necessity, upon which environmental selection then acts, unifying physics and biology at their foundations.

1. Beyond Darwin: A New Foundational Principle

While natural selection explains the adaptation and diversification of life, it doesn't fully account for the highly optimized and conserved molecular machinery at its core. Why does life use DNA with its specific helical geometry? The standard answer relies on chemical stability and "frozen accidents" of history. Recognition Physics offers a more fundamental explanation.

Recognition Optimization

We propose that before natural selection acts, a deeper principle of "Recognition Optimization" is at play. This principle states that the fundamental architectures of life are not random but are the unique, optimal solutions for information processing and stability as dictated by the physical laws of recognition.

2. The Physical Necessity of DNA's Geometry

Applying the core Recognition Physics axiom of "Minimal Overhead" to molecular information systems, we can derive the geometry of DNA from first principles, without empirical inputs. The framework's cost minimization procedures, when applied to polynucleotide interactions, uniquely yield the φ-based geometry (golden ratio) observed in B-DNA's grooves and helical pitch. This is not an accident of evolution but a mandate of physics.

3. Optimal by Design, Selected by Environment

The crucial insight is that this physically mandated, optimal geometry for DNA is not just stable—it is also inherently perfect for the functions of life. The φ-based structure is shown to produce φ-scaled enhancements in:

  • Information Fidelity: Minimizing errors in replication and transcription.
  • Energy Efficiency: Operating below standard thermodynamic limits.
  • Quantum Coherence Protection: Shielding quantum information from decoherence, explaining DNA's remarkable room-temperature quantum effects.

Recognition Physics thus provides the blueprint for the ideal information-bearing molecule. Natural selection then acts upon organisms that utilize this pre-optimized blueprint. This reframes the origin of life: physics provides the optimized architecture, and evolution explores the possibilities within it.

4. Conclusion: Unifying Physics and Biology

The "Optimized Origin" perspective does not replace Darwin but provides a deeper, physical foundation for his work. It suggests that life is not merely a product of random variation and environmental filtering, but a manifestation of universal, recognition-based optimization principles. By showing that DNA's structure is a necessary outcome of these principles, Recognition Physics offers a powerful unification of the fundamental laws of physics and the core architecture of life.
