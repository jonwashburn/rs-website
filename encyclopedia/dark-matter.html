In Plain English

Dark matter is an unseen substance that helps explain how galaxies and galaxy clusters behave. Although it cannot be seen directly, its presence is inferred from the gravitational effects it has on visible matter, such as stars and galaxies. The specific fraction of dark matter in the universe is approximately 26.49%, a value derived from complex mathematical relationships involving interference patterns.

Why It Matters

Understanding dark matter is essential for explaining the structure and dynamics of the universe. It influences galaxy formation, the motion of galaxies within clusters, and the overall expansion of the universe. Without dark matter, many observations in cosmology would be difficult to explain.

How It Works

Dark matter interacts primarily through gravity, affecting the motion of galaxies and galaxy clusters. It does not interact with electromagnetic forces, which is why it remains invisible. Common Misconceptions

One common misconception is that dark matter is the same as dark energy. While both are mysterious components of the universe, dark matter is associated with gravitational effects, whereas dark energy is linked to the accelerated expansion of the universe.

FAQs

What is dark matter made of?

The exact composition of dark matter is still unknown, but it is hypothesized to consist of particles that do not interact with electromagnetic forces, making them invisible.

How do we know dark matter exists?

Evidence for dark matter comes from various observations, including the rotation curves of galaxies, gravitational lensing, and the cosmic microwave background radiation.
