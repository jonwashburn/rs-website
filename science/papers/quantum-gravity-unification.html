Abstract

We propose that quantum mechanics and gravity emerge from a single information-processing principle: the cosmic ledger allocates finite refresh bandwidth to maintain physical states. A system remains in quantum superposition while the marginal bandwidth cost of tracking coherences is lower than the expected cost of collapsing them; a measurement event occurs precisely when this inequality reverses. Embedding the recognition-weight formalism in Einstein's field equations yields a semi-classical theory that reproduces general relativity in the high-bandwidth limit and predicts tiny, testable deviations in low-priority regimes. The framework naturally resolves the measurement problem, derives the Born rule from bandwidth optimization, and unifies "dark" phenomena with quantum collapse.

1. The Bandwidth Hypothesis

The incompatibility between quantum mechanics and general relativity is one of the greatest challenges in physics. We propose a new path to unification based on a single, powerful idea: the universe has a finite bandwidth for processing information. In this view, reality operates like a computational system with a limited refresh rate.

Quantum and Gravity from One Principle

  • Quantum Superposition: The universe maintains multiple possibilities when it lacks the bandwidth to compute a definite outcome.
  • Quantum Collapse: A measurement or "collapse" occurs when the informational cost of maintaining coherence exceeds the cost of selecting a single outcome.
  • Gravity and Dark Matter: Gravitational updates are triaged. High-priority systems (like planets) are refreshed constantly, obeying standard gravity. Low-priority systems (like distant stars in a galaxy) experience a "refresh lag," which manifests as an apparent excess of gravity—what we currently call dark matter.

2. The Cosmic Ledger and the Born Rule

The framework is built upon the eight axioms of the Recognition Science cosmic ledger, which define a discrete, balanced, and optimized information processing system. From these axioms, we derive the universe's total information bandwidth and maximum bit rate.

A key result of this paper is the derivation of the Born rule from these first principles. When the ledger must trigger a quantum collapse, it selects the outcome that minimizes the expected future bandwidth cost. We show that the optimization problem for this selection process yields the familiar probability rule:

\[ P(k) = |\langle k|\psi\rangle|^2 \]

Thus, the probabilistic nature of quantum mechanics is not a fundamental axiom but an emergent consequence of optimal information processing in a bandwidth-limited reality.

3. Field Equations and Falsifiable Predictions

We embed the "recognition-weight" field, which quantifies the local computational strain or refresh lag, into the Einstein field equations. The resulting semi-classical theory reproduces general relativity perfectly in high-bandwidth regimes (like the solar system) but predicts tiny, testable deviations in low-bandwidth, low-priority regimes.

This leads to several falsifiable predictions that can be tested with near-term experiments:

  • Pulsar Timing Arrays: A predictable timing residual of \(\sim 10\) nanoseconds due to refresh lag.
  • Atom Interferometry: A specific \(1/f\) phase noise signature caused by bandwidth discreteness.
  • Ultra-Diffuse Galaxies: A 10x suppression of the expected 21cm linewidth due to extreme refresh lag.

4. Conclusion

This framework offers a path to unifying quantum mechanics and gravity by treating both as emergent phenomena from a single, underlying principle of finite information bandwidth. It resolves the measurement problem, derives the Born rule, and provides a physical mechanism for dark matter without new particles. The theory is highly constrained and makes specific, falsifiable predictions that will be tested within the next decade. If confirmed, it suggests that reality computes itself into existence through the optimal allocation of finite resources—a profound shift in our understanding of the cosmos.
