What is the nature of evil?

From moral philosophy to physical measurement.

The Hard Problem

The nature of evil has puzzled humanity since the dawn of consciousness. Is evil an independent force opposing good? A corruption of something originally pure? The absence of good, like darkness is the absence of light? Or is it simply a human construct, a label we place on actions that harm our survival or social cohesion? These questions have generated millennia of philosophical and theological debate, but no consensus has emerged about what evil fundamentally is.

The Conventional View

Traditional approaches to evil fall into several categories. Religious perspectives often frame evil as rebellion against divine order, sin, or the work of malevolent supernatural forces. Philosophical approaches range from evil as the absence of good (the "privation theory") to evil as a necessary counterpart to good in a dualistic universe. Psychological and sociological views focus on the conditions that produce harmful behaviors—trauma, social dysfunction, evolutionary maladaptation. While these explanations describe various manifestations of evil, they fail to provide a fundamental, universal definition of what evil actually is at the deepest level of reality.
